Music has become an integral componet that is as dynamic as a person's life. Users demand a fast and flexible process to keep their music evolving at the rate of their mood and to match their environment. With a team of 3 other designers we conducted user reseach, compared tasks flows and information architecture, wireframed, and prototyping two final designs.

Formative Research

Each team member interviewed people who self identified as music listeners to learn user behaviors and motivations. The data was reccorded in a collaborative spreadsheet and we collectively classified insights and identified 9 scenarios users attempting trying to make a playlist may encounter.





Sample Questions


  • What platform do you use to listen to music and why?
  • How do you organize your music?
  • When do you listen to music and how do you decide which playlist?
  • Can you show us your playlists and why you made them?
  • What are the different ways that you add the playlists to your account?
  • What are some challenges you may face when making a playlist?
  • Can you guide us through the process of making a playlist?

User Scenarios


  • Using curated playlist based on what you like to find new music
  • Making a playlist for driving
  • Deciding which playlist to put a song when it can fit in 2 playlist
  • Making a playlist for a particular artist before a concert/festival
  • Finding a premade study playlist to concentrate on studying
  • Making a playlist to reflect a mood
  • Saving a playlist that you like but wanting to add/delete certain songs
  • Deleting songs that are no longer relevant (get skipped a lot)
  • Curating your own playlist based on songs you like from other playlist

Comparative Analysis

I was responsible for recruiting and asking users to guide me through the process of making a playlist for a particular artist before a music festival using whichever means of listening to music they most frequently use. The findings were classified either as an intuitive or complicated example depending on weather the users mental model matched the affordances of the application and they successfully achieved their intentions without hesitation.

Information Architecture

Compared information architecture from a variety of music applications to determine if the mental model of users match the task flow.

Desing One: Playlist Based on Mood


One key distinction of our design is the search bar at the top of every page. Despite what seemed to be the industry standard of having a separete search tab, we placed it in every page because the majority of the users we interviewed used the search bar as the main method to discover new music. This creates easy access to artists and songs regardless of the task at hand.


Genra and mood are key aspects that influence the music users select. One of the main challenges was including both genra and mood without overwhelming the user with too many options or multiple layers of navigability. We decided to include moods for each artist because by searching for an artist there is no need to select a genra. Further, when users search for an artist and then choose a mood it is more likely for users to hear music they like because they already know the artist.







Design Two: Selectively Copy an Existing Playlist

\

Navigation in the second design is similar to existing music applications. It abides by the industry conventions by having the users select a genra and then a specific playlist. However, the main difference is the affordance to copy all of the songs from an existing playlist. Then the user can deselect the songs they dont like and add them to their new personalized playlist. This change helps the user have more autonomy of their songs and reduces the time they spend skipping songs.
